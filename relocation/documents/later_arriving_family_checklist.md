# Later-Arriving Family Checklist
Use this with `../checklist.md` if the principal worker lands first and the spouse / child join Canada later.

Use this as a practical runbook, not as a guarantee of any specific border outcome. Officers can still ask for different supporting proof based on the exact facts on the day of travel.

## Short Answer

- later arrival is allowed
- the principal worker must enter Canada before or at the same time as family members; dependants should not arrive first
- a 25-day India trip by itself should not create a new IME requirement
- the main later-arrival gotchas are the child travel package, proof the principal already entered and can work, separate MSP clocks, and making sure goods to follow were declared on the first arrival

## Before the Principal Worker Leaves California

### Child Travel Package

- prepare a signed consent letter authorizing the child to travel to Canada with the mother
- attach a photocopy of the non-travelling parent’s signed passport or national ID
- pack the child’s birth certificate
- if applicable, pack custody papers or a death certificate
- IRCC says the permission letter does not need to be certified
- Travel.gc.ca still strongly recommends having the signatures witnessed by a notary public or similar official
- carry the original paper documents, not just phone photos
- even if no one asks for them at check-in, carry the paper consent package; IRCC notes a minor child can be refused entry if the officer is not satisfied the parents or guardians authorized the travel

### Family Relationship Documents

- give your spouse physical copies of the marriage certificate and the child’s birth certificate
- keep scanned backups in a shared folder or email thread

## After the Principal Worker Lands in Canada

- do not treat the later family flights as fully safe until your own entry is complete and your work permit is actually issued
- send your spouse a printed or scannable copy of:
  - your work permit
  - your Canadian address and phone number
  - your employment letter
  - your first Canadian paystub, if available
  - your stamped / numbered BSF186 / BSF186A copy, if you declared goods to follow
- apply for MSP as soon as you have a B.C. address
- keep the original stamped CBSA personal-effects copy; that is the key proof for later release of goods to follow

## What Your Spouse and Child Should Carry in Hand Luggage

### Core Documents

- passports
- valid visa or eTA, if required
- work permit letter of introduction for your spouse
- the child’s travel / status documents and any IRCC approval letter issued for him
- printed copy of your work permit
- marriage certificate
- child’s birth certificate
- child consent letter plus copy of your signed passport or ID
- Canadian address and phone number

### Helpful Supporting Proof

- your employment letter
- your first Canadian paystub, if available
- copy of your stamped / numbered BSF186 and goods-to-follow list, if relevant

## Medical / IME Decision Rule

- under current IRCC rules, India is on the designated-country list
- for temporary residents planning to stay more than 6 months, the main location-based trigger is having lived in or travelled in a designated country for 6 consecutive months in the year before entry
- a 25-day India trip does not meet that threshold by itself, so it should not normally create a new IME requirement
- the separate issue is job restrictions; if your spouse wants to work in health care, child care, primary / secondary school settings, or similar protected settings, an IME matters even if the India trip does not
- cleanest path: if your spouse may want one of those jobs soon, do an upfront IME before travel and carry the panel physician information sheet
- do not leave the IME to the last minute; IRCC notes that results may take around 30 days or longer to appear in the system
- if your spouse lands without a qualifying medical, check the printed work permit for occupation restrictions before leaving the airport
- if your spouse completed an older IME for a past application, do not assume it still helps unless IRCC can still see a valid medical result in the system when the permit is issued

## MSP Timing and Insurance

- later-arriving family members serve their own MSP wait period: the balance of the month they establish residence in B.C. plus 2 full calendar months
- example: if you arrive in early June and they arrive in early July, your MSP would start September 1 and theirs would start October 1
- add them to MSP as soon as they arrive, but plan private coverage until their own effective date
- do not assume employer coverage starts immediately for them; confirm it directly

## Customs / Goods to Follow

- if household goods will arrive later, they should be declared on BSF186 / BSF186A at the first point of arrival in Canada
- keep the stamped / numbered importer copy and the goods list
- send your spouse a copy in case CBSA or the mover asks how the family shipment was declared
- do not rely on later discretion to recreate a missing goods-to-follow list; the clean approach is to declare the full list on the first eligible arrival

## Landing-Day Checks for Your Spouse

- be ready to explain that the principal worker already entered Canada and is legally authorized to work
- present the letter of introduction, relationship documents, and the child travel package together instead of waiting to be asked for each item separately
- before leaving the counter, read the printed work permit and make sure the validity dates and any medical conditions are understood

## Biggest Gotchas

- family member arrives before the principal worker
- child travels with one parent but without the consent package
- assuming MSP starts on your date instead of their own
- forgetting to declare goods to follow on the first arrival
- missing a medical restriction printed on the spouse’s work permit
- relying only on phone photos instead of printed copies
